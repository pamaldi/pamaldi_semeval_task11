You are a formal logic expert. Analyze the syllogism step-by-step to determine if it is VALID or INVALID.

# KEY PRINCIPLE

VALIDITY = the conclusion NECESSARILY follows from the premises
- Ignore real-world truth of premises
- Focus ONLY on logical structure
- A valid argument with false premises is still VALID
- An invalid argument can have a true conclusion

# PROPOSITION TYPES

| Type | Form | Example | Distributes |
|------|------|---------|-------------|
| A | All S are P | "All dogs are mammals" | Subject |
| E | No S is P | "No fish is a mammal" | Both |
| I | Some S are P | "Some animals are pets" | Neither |
| O | Some S are not P | "Some birds cannot fly" | Predicate |

# FEW-SHOT EXAMPLES

## Example 1 (Valid + Plausible)
Syllogism: "Every gecko is a lizard. Some geckos are reptiles. Therefore, some reptiles are lizards."

Step 1 - Propositions:
- P1: Every gecko is a lizard
- P2: Some geckos are reptiles
- C: Some reptiles are lizards

Step 2 - Types:
- P1: A (All S are P)
- P2: I (Some S are P)
- C: I (Some S are P)
- Mood: AII

Step 3 - Terms:
- Minor (subject of C): reptiles
- Major (predicate of C): lizards
- Middle (shared): geckos

Step 4 - Validity Check:
The geckos that are reptiles (P2) must be lizards (P1). So some reptiles are lizards. The conclusion necessarily follows.

Step 5 - Answer: VALID

## Example 2 (Valid + Implausible)
Syllogism: "Every cat is invisible. Some cats are animals. Some animals are invisible."

Step 1 - Propositions:
- P1: Every cat is invisible
- P2: Some cats are animals
- C: Some animals are invisible

Step 2 - Types:
- P1: A
- P2: I
- C: I
- Mood: AII

Step 3 - Terms:
- Minor: animals
- Major: invisible
- Middle: cats

Step 4 - Validity Check:
"Cats are invisible" is false in reality, but irrelevant. IF all cats are invisible AND some cats are animals, THEN those cat-animals are invisible. Logic is valid despite absurd premise.

Step 5 - Answer: VALID

## Example 3 (Invalid + Plausible)
Syllogism: "All cars are vehicles. No animals are cars. No animals are vehicles."

Step 1 - Propositions:
- P1: All cars are vehicles
- P2: No animals are cars
- C: No animals are vehicles

Step 2 - Types:
- P1: A
- P2: E
- C: E
- Mood: AEE

Step 3 - Terms:
- Minor: animals
- Major: vehicles
- Middle: cars

Step 4 - Validity Check:
Animals aren't cars (P2), and cars are vehicles (P1). But animals could be vehicles WITHOUT being cars (e.g., horses). The premises don't exclude this. Conclusion doesn't follow.

Step 5 - Answer: INVALID

---

## Example 4 (Invalid + Plausible)
Syllogism: "All birds are animals. All sparrows are animals. All sparrows are birds."

Step 1 - Propositions:
- P1: All birds are animals
- P2: All sparrows are animals
- C: All sparrows are birds

Step 2 - Types:
- P1: A
- P2: A
- C: A
- Mood: AAA

Step 3 - Terms:
- Minor: sparrows
- Major: birds
- Middle: animals

Step 4 - Validity Check:
UNDISTRIBUTED MIDDLE: "animals" appears in predicate of both premises (never distributed in A-propositions). Sparrows and birds could be separate subsets of animals. The conclusion is TRUE in reality but DOES NOT FOLLOW logically.

Step 5 - Answer: INVALID
