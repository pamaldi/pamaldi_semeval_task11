PROMPT 1: STRUCTURE EXTRACTION (structure_extraction.txt)
================================================================================

You are a formal logic expert. Your task is to extract the logical structure from syllogisms.

## Proposition Types

| Type | Pattern | Example |
|------|---------|---------|
| A | "All S are P" | "Every dog is a mammal" |
| E | "No S is P" | "No reptile is a mammal" |
| I | "Some S are P" | "Some birds are pets" |
| O | "Some S are not P" | "Some animals are not dangerous" |

## Common Phrasings

### A-type (Universal Affirmative: All S are P)
- "All S are P"
- "Every S is a P"
- "Any S is a P"
- "Each S is a P"
- "S are always P"
- "Whatever is S is P"
- "Anything that is S is P"
- "Every single S is P"
- "S are, without exception, P"

### E-type (Universal Negative: No S is P)
- "No S is P"
- "No S are P"
- "There is no S that is P"
- "S are never P"
- "Nothing that is S is P"
- "It is not true that any S is P"
- "Not a single S is P"

### I-type (Particular Affirmative: Some S are P)
- "Some S are P"
- "A few S are P"
- "There are S that are P"
- "Certain S are P"
- "At least one S is P"
- "A portion of S are P"
- "There exist S that are P"
- "A number of S are P"
- "A select few S are P"

### O-type (Particular Negative: Some S are not P)
- "Some S are not P"
- "Not all S are P"
- "There are S that are not P"
- "A few S are not P"
- "Certain S are not P"
- "At least one S is not P"
- "Some S fail to be P"

## Syllogism Figures

The figure is determined by the position of the middle term (M):

| Figure | Premise 1 | Premise 2 |
|--------|-----------|-----------|
| 1 | M - P | S - M |
| 2 | P - M | S - M |
| 3 | M - P | M - S |
| 4 | P - M | M - S |

Where:
- M = Middle term (appears in both premises, not in conclusion)
- P = Major term (predicate of conclusion)
- S = Minor term (subject of conclusion)

## Your Task

Given a syllogism, extract:
1. Each proposition's type (A/E/I/O)
2. Each proposition's subject and predicate
3. The middle term
4. The major term (predicate of conclusion)
5. The minor term (subject of conclusion)
6. The figure (1/2/3/4)

## Output Format

Return ONLY valid JSON, no explanations:

```json
{
  "premise1": {
    "type": "A",
    "subject": "dogs",
    "predicate": "mammals",
    "raw_text": "All dogs are mammals"
  },
  "premise2": {
    "type": "A",
    "subject": "mammals",
    "predicate": "animals",
    "raw_text": "All mammals are animals"
  },
  "conclusion": {
    "type": "A",
    "subject": "dogs",
    "predicate": "animals",
    "raw_text": "All dogs are animals"
  },
  "middle_term": "mammals",
  "major_term": "animals",
  "minor_term": "dogs",
  "figure": 1,
  "mood": "AAA"
}
```

## Examples

### Example 1
Input: "Every mammal is an animal. All dogs are mammals. Therefore, all dogs are animals."

Output:
```json
{
  "premise1": {"type": "A", "subject": "mammals", "predicate": "animals", "raw_text": "Every mammal is an animal"},
  "premise2": {"type": "A", "subject": "dogs", "predicate": "mammals", "raw_text": "All dogs are mammals"},
  "conclusion": {"type": "A", "subject": "dogs", "predicate": "animals", "raw_text": "All dogs are animals"},
  "middle_term": "mammals",
  "major_term": "animals",
  "minor_term": "dogs",
  "figure": 1,
  "mood": "AAA"
}
```

### Example 2
Input: "No reptiles are mammals. Some animals are reptiles. Therefore, some animals are not mammals."

Output:
```json
{
  "premise1": {"type": "E", "subject": "reptiles", "predicate": "mammals", "raw_text": "No reptiles are mammals"},
  "premise2": {"type": "I", "subject": "animals", "predicate": "reptiles", "raw_text": "Some animals are reptiles"},
  "conclusion": {"type": "O", "subject": "animals", "predicate": "mammals", "raw_text": "Some animals are not mammals"},
  "middle_term": "reptiles",
  "major_term": "mammals",
  "minor_term": "animals",
  "figure": 1,
  "mood": "EIO"
}
```

## Important Notes

1. **Normalize terms**: Convert to singular lowercase (e.g., "Dogs" → "dog", "Mammals" → "mammal")
2. **Handle synonyms**: "creatures", "things", "beings" should be identified with their referent
3. **Identify implicit structure**: Sometimes the conclusion uses "thus", "therefore", "consequently", etc.
4. **Be precise**: The middle term MUST appear in both premises and NOT in the conclusion
5. **Mood**: Concatenate the types of premise1 + premise2 + conclusion (e.g., "AAA", "EIO")

Now extract the structure from the given syllogism.

PROMPT 2: EXTRACTION REFLEXION (extraction_reflexion.txt)
================================================================================

You are a formal logic expert. Your previous extraction attempt had errors.

## Your Task

Fix the errors identified below and provide a CORRECT extraction.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Middle Term Errors**:
   - The middle term MUST appear in BOTH premises
   - The middle term must NOT appear in the conclusion
   - Check that you identified the correct middle term

2. **Mood Errors**:
   - Mood = premise1.type + premise2.type + conclusion.type
   - Example: If premise1 is A, premise2 is E, conclusion is E → mood is "AEE"
   - Make sure the mood string matches the actual proposition types

3. **Figure Errors**:
   - Figure 1: Middle is SUBJECT of premise1, PREDICATE of premise2
   - Figure 2: Middle is PREDICATE of both premises
   - Figure 3: Middle is SUBJECT of both premises
   - Figure 4: Middle is PREDICATE of premise1, SUBJECT of premise2

4. **Proposition Type Errors**:
   - A = "All S are P" (universal affirmative)
   - E = "No S is P" (universal negative)
   - I = "Some S are P" (particular affirmative)
   - O = "Some S are not P" (particular negative)
   - Watch for tricky phrasings like "Not all" = O, "A portion of" = I

5. **Term Identification Errors**:
   - Major term = predicate of conclusion
   - Minor term = subject of conclusion
   - Normalize terms to lowercase singular form

## Output Format

Return ONLY valid JSON:

```json
{
  "premise1": {
    "type": "A|E|I|O",
    "subject": "term",
    "predicate": "term",
    "raw_text": "original text"
  },
  "premise2": {
    "type": "A|E|I|O",
    "subject": "term",
    "predicate": "term",
    "raw_text": "original text"
  },
  "conclusion": {
    "type": "A|E|I|O",
    "subject": "term",
    "predicate": "term",
    "raw_text": "original text"
  },
  "middle_term": "term that appears in both premises only",
  "major_term": "predicate of conclusion",
  "minor_term": "subject of conclusion",
  "figure": 1|2|3|4,
  "mood": "XYZ"
}
```

## Validation Checklist

Before submitting, verify:
- [ ] middle_term appears in premise1 (as subject OR predicate)
- [ ] middle_term appears in premise2 (as subject OR predicate)
- [ ] middle_term does NOT appear in conclusion
- [ ] major_term equals conclusion.predicate
- [ ] minor_term equals conclusion.subject
- [ ] mood equals premise1.type + premise2.type + conclusion.type
- [ ] figure is 1, 2, 3, or 4
- [ ] all types are A, E, I, or O

Now fix the errors and provide the correct extraction.

================================================================================
FALLACY DETECTION RULES
================================================================================

The validity checker detects these fallacies for invalid syllogisms:

1. FALLACY OF EXCLUSIVE PREMISES
   - Two negative premises (E or O)
   - No valid conclusion possible

2. FALLACY OF DRAWING AFFIRMATIVE FROM NEGATIVE
   - Negative premise (E or O) with affirmative conclusion (A or I)
   - Invalid

3. FALLACY OF DRAWING NEGATIVE FROM AFFIRMATIVES
   - Two affirmative premises (A or I) with negative conclusion (E or O)
   - Invalid

4. FALLACY OF TWO PARTICULARS
   - Two particular premises (I or O)
   - No valid conclusion possible

5. ILLICIT PROCESS
   - Particular premise with universal conclusion
   - Invalid

6. UNDISTRIBUTED MIDDLE
   - Middle term not distributed in any premise
   - Distribution rules:
     - A: Subject distributed
     - E: Both distributed
     - I: Neither distributed
     - O: Predicate distributed

7. ILLICIT MAJOR / ILLICIT MINOR
   - Term distributed in conclusion but not in premises
   - Major term = predicate of conclusion
   - Minor term = subject of conclusion

================================================================================
FIGURE VERIFICATION EXPLANATION
================================================================================

Figure verification is a dedicated, low-cost LLM call that explicitly verifies
the figure calculation after the initial extraction.

WHY IT'S NEEDED:
- The LLM often correctly identifies the middle term
- But then miscalculates the figure from this information
- The figure is critical because the same mood can be valid in one figure
  and invalid in another (e.g., AEE-2 is valid, AEE-1 is invalid)

HOW IT WORKS:
1. After initial extraction, build a focused prompt asking:
   - "Where is the middle term in Premise 1? (SUBJECT or PREDICATE)"
   - "Where is the middle term in Premise 2? (SUBJECT or PREDICATE)"
2. Use the figure table to compute the correct figure
3. If different from original, update the extraction

FIGURE TABLE:
| M in Premise 1 | M in Premise 2 | Figure |
|----------------|----------------|--------|
| SUBJECT        | PREDICATE      | 1      |
| PREDICATE      | PREDICATE      | 2      |
| SUBJECT        | SUBJECT        | 3      |
| PREDICATE      | SUBJECT        | 4      |

COST:
- Adds 1 API call per syllogism
- Very short response (~200 tokens)
- Can be combined with self-consistency

PROMPT 5: VALIDITY FALLBACK (validity_fallback.txt)
================================================================================

You are a formal logic expert. Determine if this syllogism is logically VALID or INVALID.

## Syllogism

{syllogism_text}

## Instructions

A syllogism is VALID if the conclusion NECESSARILY follows from the premises, regardless of whether the statements are true in the real world.

Focus ONLY on logical structure, NOT on real-world truth or plausibility.

### Step-by-step analysis:

1. Identify the three statements (Premise 1, Premise 2, Conclusion)
2. Identify the three terms:
   - Middle term (appears in both premises, not in conclusion)
   - Major term (predicate of conclusion)
   - Minor term (subject of conclusion)
3. Determine the type of each statement:
   - A: "All S are P" (universal affirmative)
   - E: "No S is P" (universal negative)
   - I: "Some S are P" (particular affirmative)
   - O: "Some S are not P" (particular negative)
4. Determine the figure (position of middle term):
   - Figure 1: M-P, S-M (middle is subject of P1, predicate of P2)
   - Figure 2: P-M, S-M (middle is predicate of both)
   - Figure 3: M-P, M-S (middle is subject of both)
   - Figure 4: P-M, M-S (middle is predicate of P1, subject of P2)
5. Check if the mood-figure combination is valid

### Valid syllogism forms (24 total):

Figure 1: AAA (Barbara), EAE (Celarent), AII (Darii), EIO (Ferio)
Figure 2: EAE (Cesare), AEE (Camestres), EIO (Festino), AOO (Baroco)
Figure 3: IAI (Disamis), AII (Datisi), OAO (Bocardo), EIO (Ferison)
Figure 4: AEE (Camenes), IAI (Dimaris), EIO (Fresison)

Plus 9 weakened forms (AAI, EAO variants).

### Common fallacies (INVALID):

- Undistributed middle: Middle term not distributed in either premise
- Illicit major: Major term distributed in conclusion but not in major premise
- Illicit minor: Minor term distributed in conclusion but not in minor premise
- Two negative premises: Cannot derive valid conclusion from two negatives
- Negative premise with affirmative conclusion: Invalid
- Two particular premises: Cannot derive valid conclusion

### Distribution rules:
- A (All S are P): S is distributed, P is not
- E (No S is P): Both S and P are distributed
- I (Some S are P): Neither is distributed
- O (Some S are not P): Only P is distributed

## Output

After your analysis, respond with ONLY one word on the last line:

VALID

or

INVALID


================================================================================
FALLBACK EXPLANATION
================================================================================

The LLM Fallback is used when structure extraction fails (even after reflexion).
Instead of returning no prediction, the fallback asks the LLM to directly
evaluate validity using chain-of-thought reasoning.

WHY IT'S NEEDED:
- Some syllogisms have complex/ambiguous phrasing that extraction can't handle
- Without fallback, extraction failures = 0% accuracy on those items
- With fallback, we get ~70-80% accuracy on failed extractions

HOW IT WORKS:
1. Extraction fails after max reflexion attempts
2. Fallback prompt asks LLM to analyze the syllogism step-by-step
3. LLM identifies terms, types, figure, and checks validity
4. Response is parsed for VALID/INVALID
5. If unclear, defaults to INVALID (conservative)

CONFIDENCE:
- Symbolic validation: 95% confidence (deterministic rules)
- Fallback validation: 70% confidence (LLM reasoning)

SELF-CONSISTENCY FOR FALLBACK:
- Can optionally use self-consistency voting for fallback
- Runs multiple evaluations with graduated temperatures
- Majority vote determines final prediction
- Improves fallback accuracy at cost of more API calls
