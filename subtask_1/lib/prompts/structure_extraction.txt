## ⚠️ CRITICAL: Distinguishing I-type from O-type

**This is the most common error. Pay VERY close attention!**

| Type | Pattern | Key Indicator | Example |
|------|---------|---------------|---------|
| **I** | "Some S are P" | NO negation | "Some birds are pets" |
| **O** | "Some S are NOT P" | HAS negation | "Some birds are **not** pets" |

### O-type Indicators (NEGATIVE particular) - Look for these words:
- "Some X are **not** Y"
- "There are X that are **not** Y"
- "**Not all** X are Y"
- "**Not every** X is Y"
- "A portion of X are **not** Y"
- "At least one X is **not** Y"
- "Some X **fail to be** Y"
- "There exist X which are **not** Y"
- "There are some X that are **not** Y"
- "There are a number of X who are **not** Y"

### I-type Indicators (AFFIRMATIVE particular) - NO negation present:
- "Some X are Y" (no negation!)
- "There are X that are Y"
- "A portion of X are Y"
- "At least one X is Y"
- "Some X are also Y"

### How to Check:
1. Find the copula (are/is)
2. Look for "not" AFTER the copula
3. If "not" present → **Type O**
4. If "not" absent → **Type I**

### Examples:
- "Some dogs are mammals" → **I** (no "not")
- "Some dogs are not cats" → **O** (has "not")
- "There are birds that are not fish" → **O** (has "not")
- "Not all cars are red" → **O** (negation of universal = particular negative)
- "There are some objects that are not furniture" → **O** (has "not")
- "There are a number of humans who are not robots" → **O** (has "not")

## Proposition Types

| Type | Pattern | Example |
|------|---------|---------|
| A | "All S are P" | "Every dog is a mammal" |
| E | "No S is P" | "No reptile is a mammal" |
| I | "Some S are P" | "Some birds are pets" |
| O | "Some S are not P" | "Some animals are not dangerous" |

- "All S are P"
- "Every S is a P"

- "Each S is a P"
- "S are always P"
- "Whatever is S is P"

- "Every single S is P"
- "S are, without exception, P"

### E-type (Universal Negative: No S is P)
- "No S is P"
- "No S are P"
- "There is no S that is P"
- "S are never P"
- "Nothing that is S is P"
- "It is not true that any S is P"
- "Not a single S is P"
- "S are in no way P"
- "S cannot be P"

### I-type (Particular Affirmative: Some S are P)
- "Some S are P"
- "A few S are P"
- "There are S that are P"
- "Certain S are P"
- "At least one S is P"
- "A portion of S are P"
- "There exist S that are P"
- "A number of S are P"
- "A select few S are P"

### O-type (Particular Negative: Some S are not P)
- "Some S are not P"

- "A few S are not P"

## HANDLING COMPLEX TERMS

Some sentences have nested structures. Treat these as SINGLE TERMS:

| Pattern | How to Parse | Subject | Predicate |
|---------|--------------|---------|-----------|
| "Any X who is Y is Z" | All (X who is Y) are Z | X who is Y | Z |
| "Every X which is Y is Z" | All (X which is Y) are Z | X which is Y | Z |
| "Any X that is Y is Z" | All (X that is Y) are Z | X that is Y | Z |
| "Things that are X are Y" | All (things that are X) are Y | things that are X | Y |

**Example:**
- "Any person who is a doctor is wealthy" 
- Subject = "person who is a doctor"
- Predicate = "wealthy"
- Type = A

**IMPORTANT:** When the same complex term appears in multiple places, it should be normalized to a simpler form for comparison. For example:
- "things that are potatoes" = "potato"
- "creatures that are dogs" = "dog"
- "person who is a doctor" = "doctor" (if doctor appears elsewhere)

## STEP-BY-STEP EXTRACTION PROCEDURE

Follow these steps IN ORDER. Do not skip steps.

### STEP 1: Identify the three sentences

Separate the syllogism into exactly three parts:
- Sentence 1 = First premise
- Sentence 2 = Second premise
- Sentence 3 = Conclusion (marked by "therefore", "thus", "consequently", "it follows", "hence", "it must be the case", etc.)

### STEP 2: Extract the CONCLUSION first

Analyze the conclusion sentence:
- Identify its SUBJECT → this is the **MINOR TERM (S)**
- Identify its PREDICATE → this is the **MAJOR TERM (P)**
- Determine its type (A/E/I/O)

### STEP 3: Identify the MIDDLE TERM (M)

The middle term is the term that:
- Appears in BOTH premises
- Does **NOT** appear in the conclusion

To find it:
1. List all terms in premise 1
2. List all terms in premise 2
3. List all terms in conclusion (these are S and P)
4. Find the term that is in both premise lists but NOT in the conclusion list

**Critical check**: If S or P appears in both premises, you may have misidentified the terms. Re-check.

### STEP 4: Analyze each premise

For Premise 1:
- Identify subject and predicate
- Determine type (A/E/I/O)
- **Explicitly note**: Is M the SUBJECT or the PREDICATE of this premise?

For Premise 2:
- Identify subject and predicate
- Determine type (A/E/I/O)
- **Explicitly note**: Is M the SUBJECT or the PREDICATE of this premise?

### STEP 5: Calculate the FIGURE (CRITICAL STEP)

**This is where most errors occur. Be very careful.**

First, determine M's position in each premise:
- In Premise 1: Is M the SUBJECT or PREDICATE?
- In Premise 2: Is M the SUBJECT or PREDICATE?

Then use this lookup table:

| M in Premise 1 | M in Premise 2 | Figure |
|----------------|----------------|--------|
| SUBJECT | PREDICATE | **1** |
| PREDICATE | PREDICATE | **2** |
| SUBJECT | SUBJECT | **3** |
| PREDICATE | SUBJECT | **4** |

**Memory aid:**
- Figure 1: M goes "down" (subject→predicate) = S-P pattern
- Figure 2: M stays "right" (predicate→predicate) = P-P pattern
- Figure 3: M stays "left" (subject→subject) = S-S pattern
- Figure 4: M goes "up" (predicate→subject) = P-S pattern

### STEP 6: Build the MOOD

Mood = Premise1.type + Premise2.type + Conclusion.type

Example: If P1=A, P2=E, Conclusion=E → Mood = "AEE"

### STEP 7: Final verification

Before outputting, verify ALL of these:
- [ ] M appears in premise1 (as subject OR predicate)
- [ ] M appears in premise2 (as subject OR predicate)
- [ ] M does NOT appear in conclusion
- [ ] S equals conclusion.subject
- [ ] P equals conclusion.predicate
- [ ] Figure matches M's positions from Step 5
- [ ] Mood equals the three types concatenated

**Input:** "All cars are vehicles. No animal is a car. Therefore, no animal is a vehicle."

- Conclusion: "No animal is a vehicle" (marked by "Therefore")

### STEP 2 - Conclusion analysis:
- Conclusion: "No animal is a vehicle"
- Subject of conclusion: **animal** → MINOR TERM (S)
- Predicate of conclusion: **vehicle** → MAJOR TERM (P)
- Type: **E** (No S is P)

### STEP 3 - Find Middle Term:
- Terms in premise 1: {car, vehicle}
- Terms in premise 2: {animal, car}
- Terms in conclusion: {animal, vehicle}
- Term in both premises but NOT in conclusion: **car** → MIDDLE TERM (M)

### STEP 4 - Premise analysis:
- Premise 1: "All cars are vehicles"
  - Subject: car (this is M)
  - Predicate: vehicle (this is P)
  - Type: A
  - **M (car) is SUBJECT**
  
- Premise 2: "No animal is a car"
  - Subject: animal (this is S)
  - Predicate: car (this is M)
  - Type: E
  - **M (car) is PREDICATE**

### STEP 5 - Calculate Figure:
- M in Premise 1: **SUBJECT**
- M in Premise 2: **PREDICATE**
- Lookup: SUBJECT + PREDICATE = **FIGURE 1**

### STEP 6 - Build Mood:
- P1 type: A
- P2 type: E
- Conclusion type: E
- Mood: **AEE**

### STEP 7 - Verify:

- [x] M (car) in premise2? Yes, as predicate
- [x] M (car) in conclusion? No
- [x] S (animal) = conclusion.subject? Yes
- [x] P (vehicle) = conclusion.predicate? Yes
- [x] Figure 1 matches? Yes (M=subj in P1, M=pred in P2)
- [x] Mood AEE matches types? Yes (A+E+E)

## CRITICAL: Distinguishing Proposition Types

| Keyword | Type | Example |
|---------|------|---------|
| "Every X is Y" | **A** | "Every bird is an animal" |
| "All X are Y" | **A** | "All birds are animals" |
| "No X is Y" | **E** | "No bird is a fish" |
| "Some X are Y" | **I** | "Some birds are pets" |
| "Some X are not Y" | **O** | "Some birds are not pets" |
| "Not all X are Y" | **O** | "Not all birds fly" |

**WARNING:** 
- "Every X is Y" is ALWAYS type A (affirmative), never E
- "Some X are not Y" is ALWAYS type O (negative), not I
- The negation is in the COPULA ("are not"), not in the predicate
